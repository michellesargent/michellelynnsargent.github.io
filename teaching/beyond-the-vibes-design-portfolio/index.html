Beyond the Vibes: Owning Your Design Portfolio

Vibe coding can get you a prototype in an afternoon. A dashboard, a landing page, a concept you can click through and share. That's genuinely exciting, and genuinely incomplete as a portfolio strategy.

At Cisco's annual UX design conference, dzone, I led a workshop called "Beyond the Vibes: Owning Your Design Portfolio." The session was for designers who know they need a portfolio, feel the pull of AI-generated demos, and aren't sure how to move from I made a thing with prompts to I own a body of work that represents me.

The problem

AI tools make creation feel effortless. Describe an app, get an app. But hiring managers and collaborators aren't looking for proof that you can prompt: they're looking for proof of judgment, craft, curation, and the ability to ship.

A portfolio built only on vibe-coded prototypes has three gaps:

  1. No ownership narrative. Who made the decisions? What was hard? What did you learn?
  2. No local development fluency. Everything lives in a sandbox. You can't show how you set up, iterated, or maintained work.
  3. No curation. A pile of AI demos isn't a portfolio. A portfolio is an argument about what you do and why it matters.

How I designed the session

dzone is Cisco's annual UX design conference, community, inspiration, upskilling, and network building. In 2025 I helped organize the NYC event alongside Danielle Santiago-Sparandera, Michelle L., and Joanna Yeh, and presented this workshop as part of the program.

I structured the session around a shift in mindset:

Vibe coding portfolio Owned portfolio
AI generates the work You curate and frame the work
Links to sandboxes Local environment you control
"Look what I made" "Here's my role, process, and outcome"
Speed as the proof Judgment as the proof

The workshop walked attendees through:

  • Why local development matters: even for designers. Setting up a project on your machine means you understand the artifact, not just the output.
  • How to curate, not collect: selecting 4–5 strong case studies instead of every project you touched
  • Portfolio as practice: the act of building and maintaining your own site is itself design-engineering proof

I shared my own development journey openly, including the mentors and peers who helped me get there. Meg Griffin's guidance was instrumental in moving from "I use AI tools" to "I own a local development workflow."

The methodology

From vibes to ownership

Vibe coding is a valid starting point. I use Figma Make, Copilot, and Cursor constantly. But a portfolio needs to show what happens after the first prompt:

  • How you set up a local environment
  • How you version-control iterations
  • How you document decisions
  • How you choose what to show and what to leave out

The workshop connected directly to practices I teach elsewhere: moving from Figma Make to GitHub, using annotations as handoff documentation, treating markdown files as structured context for AI assistants. The portfolio is the capstone: the place where all of those skills converge.

Portfolio as design-engineering proof

For designers moving toward design engineering, UX engineering, or design technologist roles, the portfolio site itself is evidence. It doesn't have to be a complex application. It needs to demonstrate:

  • Taste in layout and typography
  • Intentional information architecture
  • At least one case study with real process depth
  • Signs that you built it, not just generated it

I'm building my own portfolio along these lines, this repository is part of that practice. The work/ folder structure, case study drafts, and teaching articles are the curated body of work the workshop advocates for.

Community as accelerator

The dzone session wasn't solo instruction. It was part of a day that included talks on creativity, pattern recognition, and building creative practice from Francesca Rodriguez Sawaya and KC Lathrop. The energy in the room, designers sharing experiments, asking honest questions about AI, reinforced that portfolio ownership is a community skill, not a solitary one.

Outcomes

The workshop joined a broader dzone program that drew strong participation and discussion. Attendees left with a framework for evaluating their current portfolio against the "owned" standard, and a clear next step: set up a local project, choose one case study, and build from there.

For me, presenting the workshop accelerated my own portfolio work. Teaching ownership forced me to practice it. The case studies, teaching articles, and site structure in this repository are direct outputs of that commitment.

What you can take away

If you're a designer building or refreshing your portfolio in the age of AI:

  1. Vibe coding is a draft, not a destination. Use AI to explore. Use curation to publish.
  2. Set up locally at least once. Clone a repo, run a dev server, make a change, commit it. That experience changes how you talk about your work.
  3. Curate ruthlessly. Four strong case studies beat twenty sandbox links. Each one should answer: what was the problem, what did I do, what happened?
  4. Your portfolio can be proof of hybrid practice. You don't need to be a full-stack engineer. You need to show design intent executed with technical fluency.

The vibes get you started. Ownership is what makes the portfolio yours.
